The Idea Behind Pictures

Pictures that contain mysterious elements lead people to be active viewers. No matter which device or lens you use, solving a puzzle is a big part of the allure for most professional photographers. Viewers will feel great deal of accomplishment and appreciation when unraveling the hidden message in your image. Crafting a journey of thought and feeling for your audience can be rewarding exercise both for you and for them.

"Sight is the quickest action a human can perform, but at the same time, we only see a single object." Leonardo da Vinci, A Treatise on Painting

Controlling the Direction of Gaze

Framing

Frames emphasize the overall importance of the objects located within them. The framing technique can be useful when attempting to take an original shot of something very well-known. The Eiffel Tower, for example, can look much more interesting when photographed through a window. Arches, shutters, doors, and holes can all serve as a frame. If the picture is especially attention-grabbing, you can make the frame thinner. On the other hand, if it's just a picture of a blue sky, thicker frames can be appreciated for their own value.

Nighttime Smile, Moscow

Veiling

Semi-transparent content placed in the foreground can also attract a viewer's attention. Hazy or foggy effects, for example, can help the artist redirect the spectator's eye from the front to the back of the image. The part of the image with the most emphasis when using a technique such as this is generally one that's hidden. Slightly hiding our subjects creates a visual appeal, drawing out the audience's curiosity.

Volleyball in the Forest, Serebryany Bor

Vector indication

Vectors in photography act as visual guides for the eye. They can be in the form of lines, or a difference in lighting, color, or sharpness. These lure the spectator to observe what's important. For more optimal results, it's good to sometimes use two different kinds of vectors that will eventually level each other out. In this particular case, the onlooker will follow one vector to the very end before returning back to the beginning alongside the other. It's a fun little trip around the picture that leads you to make a series of fascinating discoveries along the way. Vectors direct attention toward the point where they converge or diverge.

Flare, Moscow

One of the most effective vectors you can use is one that focuses on the direction in which someone in the picture is looking, a technique commonly used in Renaissance artwork. Two intersecting glares can tell you a lot about the subjects' relationship. Alternatively, glimpses that meet somewhere can also point out what's most important in an image. Viewers will happily follow whichever method you choose in an attempt to solve the riddle.

Sports Ground, Votkinsk

Managing calculations

As surprising as it may sound, people are always counting to themselves and looking for geometric shapes all around them. Finding such a geometric shape hidden in a picture makes the viewer feel like a detective. It's an exhilarating flow of positive emotions that can linger long after looking at the image.

Triangles

Triangles made up of random elements stand out. Our imagination even likes making up nonexistent triangles.

Winter Sports, Vesennyaya Station

Strong curves

The graphical perfection of the parabola and hyperbola is impressive.

After the Rain, Poklonnaya Hill

Parallel lines

They happen so rarely in nature that you want to find out who put them there.

Metro Sunset, Moscow

Decorations

It's always fun to discover decorations made out of similar objects.

September, Moscow

Rhythm

Identical objects receding off into the background let you estimate distance and height. Viewers always appreciate this.

Geometry of Space, Sochi

Reflections

Everyone likes trying to figure out which side of the picture is the reflection. That's especially fun when there are multiple reflections.

Landmark, Moscow

Managing emotions

The emotional background is created subconsciously. If you analyze the contents, the dominant color exerts a psychological influence beyond your control. The person behind the shot can create a certain mood by selecting the colors.

Red

The color of desire and passion. The red color carries with it an energy, life force, and danger all at the same time.

Riga Express, Moscow

Yellow

A color that boosts the mood and appetite. Everything that's yellow attracts the viewer with a smile that says life is beautiful.

Glass Distortion, Izhevsk

Green

The color of harmony and growth. Bright green is perfect for children playing out in nature, creating the impression of growing in safety. It's no surprise that the signal to go is a green light.

Morning Practice, Moscow

Blue

The color of confidence and trust. Unlike yellow, blue pulls away from the viewer, creating a light sense of sadness.

Airport, Izhevsk

Purple

This color creates an impression of dignity, greatness (especially with a scarlet shade), self-sufficiency, and creativity.

Sunset Port, Novorossiysk

White

The color white means different things depending on the culture, though it most often stands for goodness, light, and life, including eternal life beyond the grave.

Winter River, Veretyevo

Black

Black often comes with the idea of evil and death, though as the color of clothing and electronics it more frequently gives an impression of order, elegance, and mysterious power.

Embankment, Moscow

Share!

Mobile pictures double up on the fun — a great original shot followed by the endless social media conversations about it.

"Painting with others is better than painting alone — jealousy pushes you to be among those more praised than you, the praise of others spurring you on all the while." Leonardo da Vinci, A Treatise on Painting

Don't forget to share your work since the number of likes and questions you get will tell you what you're doing right. And when you get enough shots, you can look back at them and learn something new about yourself. For example, ask yourself what it is about the ideas you have that attracts you. What do you think about the things you take pictures of? How has your attitude changed with the passing of time, and how have you yourself changed? You may find the answers in your work.
